## Title

High School Students' Consumer Self-Perception and Simulated Checkout Choices

Student: Zhiyan Zhang

Research Advisor: Lawted Wu

## Research Question & Hypothesis

Research Question: How does an interactive checkout simulation affect high school students' awareness of gaps between their consumer self-perception and simulated choices?

Hypothesis: I hypothesize that after using the interactive checkout simulation, high school students will increase their awareness of gaps between their consumer self-perception and simulated choices.

## Background

Many high school students use digital platforms every day. They see shopping pages, short videos, membership offers, game items, and online reviews. These pages are not neutral. They often include time pressure, default choices, high ratings, and other design features.

My project starts from a simple problem. Many students may believe that they make careful consumer decisions. They may say that they check rules, read reviews, and notice extra costs. But when they are placed inside a realistic checkout page, they may not always act in the same way.

This matters because digital consumption is now part of everyday life for teenagers. It is not only about money. It is also about self-understanding. A student may think, "I am careful," but a page design may reveal that their actual choice is more complicated.

My project is for high school students. It does not label users as rational or irrational. Instead, it helps them compare what they think they do with what they choose in a simulated checkout situation.

## Literature Review

Rozendaal et al. (2011) argue that advertising literacy is not always enough to protect young people from advertising effects. A young person may know that a message is persuasive, but this knowledge may not be used in the moment. This supports my project because my tool compares what students say they know with what they do in a simulated choice.

De Jans, Hudders, and Cauberghe (2018) study adolescents' self-reported advertising literacy. Their work shows that teenagers can describe their own ability to recognize and resist advertising. However, self-report alone does not show what they do in a real or simulated situation. My project adds an interactive checkout task after the self-assessment.

Donaldson and Grant-Vallone (2002) explain that self-report data can be biased. People may present themselves in a better way, or they may not fully understand their own behavior. This is important for my project because I do not only ask students what kind of consumers they think they are. I also record their choices inside the demo.

Mathur et al. (2019) study dark patterns on shopping websites. They show that online shopping pages can include design patterns such as urgency, social proof, and hidden costs. My project does not copy a real platform, but it uses similar page mechanisms in a safe and simplified way.

Steiner, Atzmuller, and Su (2017) discuss how to design reliable vignette experiments. Their work is useful because my checkout page is a kind of short scenario. It should be simple, controlled, and not overloaded with too many variables.

Braun and Clarke (2006) introduce thematic analysis. This helps my project because users will write short explanations for their choices. I can group these explanations into themes such as "I did not notice the change," "I wanted to keep the discount," or "I trusted the rating."

The gap in the literature is that many studies focus on advertising, persuasion, or consumer behavior in general. My project focuses on one smaller question: can a short interactive tool make the gap between consumer self-perception and simulated choice visible for high school students?

## Research Design / Method

My artifact is a single-page interactive website. It has two parts.

First, users answer neutral self-assessment questions. The questions do not ask, "Will you be influenced by marketing?" Instead, they ask about decision rules. For example, users answer whether they usually check conditions, notice default options, and read more information when reviews conflict.

Second, users enter a simulated checkout page. The page does not show a real product name or a real price. It uses a neutral "Order A" and a virtual budget bar. This reduces the effect of product preference and different money sensitivity.

The checkout demo has three situations:

1. A discount is about to expire.
2. An extra service is selected by default.
3. The overall rating is high, but some negative reviews appear.

In each situation, users choose an action and write one short reason. The system then compares their self-assessment with their simulated choice.

I plan to invite high school students to use the website. I will collect four kinds of data:

1. Their self-assessment answers.
2. Their choices in the three checkout situations.
3. Their short written reasons.
4. Their final reflection after seeing the result page.

For quantitative analysis, I will count how many times each user shows a mismatch between self-assessment and simulated choice. I will also compare which page mechanism creates more mismatch: discount pressure, default add-on, or review conflict.

For qualitative analysis, I will use thematic analysis. I will code the short reasons into themes, such as "did not notice," "wanted the discount," "trusted the rating," or "wanted to check more."

## Expected Results — user study not yet run

I expect that some students will show consistency. They may say that they check information, and then they may also check rules, cancel default options, or read more reviews in the demo.

I also expect that some students will show mismatch. For example, a student may say that they usually check rules, but they may continue when the discount is about to expire. Another student may say that they notice default options, but they may keep the extra service without checking it.

I expect the result page to help users see this gap more clearly. The main result I expect is not that users become "more rational" immediately. The main expected result is that they can notice where their self-perception and simulated choices differ.

I also expect the written reasons to show different patterns. Some users may say they did not notice the page change. Some may say they wanted to keep the offer. Some may say they trusted the rating. These explanations will help me understand why the mismatch happens.

No user study has been run yet. I will not report any user numbers, percentages, charts, or final conclusions at this stage.
